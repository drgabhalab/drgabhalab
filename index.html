Anal Cancer

Anal cancer, which develops in the anal canal, is an unusual kind of cancer. Stool exits the body through a small tube called the anal canal, which is located at the end of the rectum. Rectal haemorrhage and anal soreness are a couple of the indicators and symptoms of anal cancer. Anal cancer is closely related to a sexually transmitted infection called human papillomavirus (HPV).

Colon Cancer

The big intestine is where colon cancer typically first manifests itself (colon). Small, benign (noncancerous) cell clusters called polyps commonly grow on the interior of the colon as the first signs of the condition. Because of this, medical professionals advise routine screening exams to help prevent colon cancer.

Brain Tumour

A lump or growth of abnormal cells in your brain is known as a brain tumour. Primary brain tumours are those that start in the brain; secondary (metastatic) brain tumours are those that start in other regions of the body and spread to the brain. These can either be static or malignant.

Blood Cancer

Changes (mutations) in the DNA of blood cells are the root cause of all blood malignancies. The blood cells begin to behave strangely as a result of this. The possible symptoms could be chest pain, frequent infections, persistent weakness, that should not be ignored. Some blood cancer patients might not exhibit any symptoms until the condition has progressed.

Mouth Cancer

In most simple terms, any cancer that appears in any of the mouth's constituent parts is referred to as mouth cancer (oral cavity). An appointment with a doctor is required if there are persistent signs like mouth pain, ear pain, difficulty swallowing, or non-healing mouth or lip sore.

Prostate Cancer

Cancer that develops in the prostate is known as prostate cancer. In males, the prostate is a little walnut-shaped gland that secretes seminal fluid, which feeds and carries sperm. In the prostate gland, where they may not do much harm, many prostate tumours develop slowly and are localised.

Stomach Cancer

Gastric cancer, also known as stomach cancer, is a cellular tumour that begins in the stomach. Food digestion and breakdown are assisted by the stomach. It is more difficult to treat stomach cancer that penetrates the stomach wall or spreads to other organs.

Tongue Cancer

A type of cancer that starts in the cells of the tongue is called tongue cancer. The tongue can be impacted by a variety of cancers, although tongue cancer most frequently starts in the thin, flat squamous cells that cover the tongue's surface. The prognosis and course of treatment for tongue cancer are influenced by the type of cells involved.

Breast Cancer

Breast cancer is a condition in which the breast's cells proliferate out of control. Breast cancer comes in several forms. Which breast cells develop into cancer determine the type of breast cancer. Blood and lymph vessels are two ways that breast cancer can travel outside of the breast. Breast cancer is said to have metastasized when it spreads to other body regions.

Gallbladder Cancer

Just below your liver on the right side of your abdomen is your gallbladder, a little, pear-shaped organ. Bile, a digestive fluid produced by your liver, is kept in the gallbladder. A malignant cell growth that starts in the gallbladder is known as gallbladder cancer. Because gallbladder cancer frequently exhibits no particular signs or symptoms, it might not be identified until it has progressed.

Liver Cancer

Liver is an organ about the size of a football, located in the upper right part of the abdomen, above the stomach and below the diaphragm. The liver is prone to developing a number of cancers. Hepatocellular carcinoma, the most prevalent form of liver cancer, develops in the primary liver cell type (hepatocyte).

Ovarian Cancer

The cells in ovaries can penetrate and obliterate healthy biological tissues and proliferate gradually during ovarian cancer. There are two ovaries in the female reproductive system, one on either side of the uterus. The hormones progesterone and oestrogen are also produced in the ovaries, which are each roughly the size of an almond.

Throat Cancer

Behind the nose and ending in the neck is a muscular tube that is called throat. The flat cells that line the throat's inside are where throat cancer typically starts. These could be oropharyngeal, nasopharyngeal, hypopharyngeal, subglottic, and supraglottic.

Tonsil Cancer

The immune system of the body includes two oval-shaped pads at the back of your mouth called tonsils. Swallowing issues and a feeling that something is stuck in your throat can both be brought on by tonsil cancer. Tonsil cancer is frequently discovered after it has spread to surrounding tissues, such the lymph nodes in the neck.

Kidney Cancer

Two bean-shaped organs, each around the size of your fist, make up the kidneys. One kidney is situated on either side of your spine, and they are situated behind your abdominal organs. Renal cell carcinoma is the most prevalent form of kidney cancer in adults. Issues like blood in urine, fatigue, loss of appetite, are observed.

Lung Cancer

The lungs are two pliable organs located in the chest that allow you to breathe in oxygen and exhale carbon dioxide. The largest cause of cancer-related fatalities worldwide is lung cancer. Lung cancer can affect persons who have never smoked, but smokers are at a higher risk than nonsmokers.

Pancreatic Cancer

The pancreas is an organ in the abdomen that is located behind the lower stomach. The pancreas generates hormones that help to control the blood sugar as well as enzymes that help with digestion. The pancreas can develop both malignant and non-cancerous tumours.

The cells that lining the ducts that expel digestive enzymes from the pancreas are where the most prevalent type of pancreatic cancer first develops, that is, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma.

Thyroid Cancer

A butterfly-shaped gland called the thyroid can be found near the base of the neck, just below the Adam's apple. The thyroid gland makes hormones that control body temperature, weight, blood pressure, heart rate, and blood flow. Initially, thyroid cancer may not show any signs but as it develops, it may show symptoms like neck enlargement, voice changes, and trouble swallowing.

Uterus Cancer

Endometrial cancer, uterine sarcoma, and other extremely rare uterine cancers can all be referred to as uterine cancer. The endometrium, or inner lining of the uterus, is where endometrial cancer begins and it is the most common gynecologic cancer. The muscle wall of the uterus, called the myometrium, is where uterine sarcoma grows rarely.

Bile Duct Cancer

A specific type of cancer known as cholangiocarcinoma develops in the thin tubes (bile ducts) that transport the digesting fluid bile. The small intestine and the gallbladder are linked by bile ducts in the liver. Although it can happen at any age, cholangiocarcinoma, also known as bile duct cancer, mostly affects adults over the age of 50.

Cervical Cancer

The lower portion of the uterus that attaches to the vagina, or the cervix, is where the cells of cervical cancer develop. The majority of cervical cancers are brought on by different strains of the sexually transmitted infection known as the human papillomavirus (HPV). By getting screening tests and an anti-HPV vaccine, one can lower the risk of developing cervical cancer.

Lymphoma Cancer

A cancer of the lymphatic system, which is a component of the body's defence against infection, is lymphoma. The spleen, thymus gland, lymph nodes (lymph glands), and bone marrow are all components of the lymphatic system. All of those locations, as well as other organs throughout the body, can be impacted by lymphoma.

Ethmoid Sinus Cancer

A very uncommon paranasal sinus cancer is ethmoid sinus carcinoma.

It has a low incidence rate, a wide range of histologic kinds, and several different therapeutic options. Small hollow areas surrounding the nose are known as paranasal sinuses. The sinuses are lined with cells that produce mucus, which prevents the nose from drying out.

Multiple Myeloma

A malignancy known as multiple myeloma develops in a type of white blood cell known as a plasma cell. Cancerous plasma cells build up in the bone marrow and crowd out healthy blood cells in multiple myeloma.

Cancer cells create aberrant proteins that can lead to difficulties rather than beneficial antibodies.
